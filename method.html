Method

How we know

The boundary

Every feature is clipped to the historical district polygon (OSM relation -3797166) before counting, so the numbers describe a fixed area through the decade.

The 2018→19 “mapping leap” is mapping, not building

Road ways tripled in one year because a community mapping effort traced the existing alley mesh — the alleys were already there; OpenStreetMap caught up. This is a story about a place becoming legible, not about new construction.

The 2023 shop dip is a data artifact

Mapped shops fall ~253 → 173 around 2023. Shops did not close — a retagging/import change in OSM moved some features between shop=* and other tags. Treat the shop curve's late wobble as noise, not neighbourhood decline.

Café overtook fuel — but café is not #1

Under the precise district clip, cafés climbed from rank 8 to rank 2 (count 6 → 62) while fuel stayed flat (~24). Café genuinely overtook fuel. But the single most-mapped amenity in 2025 is restaurants (77), not cafés. An earlier coarse whole-snapshot probe suggested “café is #1”; the precise clip tells the truer, smaller story.

The streetlife "reality layer" is Overture, mostly Facebook

The haze of ~5,500 food & drink venues comes from the Overture Maps places theme (CDLA-Permissive-2.0) — ~99% of those rows are sourced from Meta, i.e. Facebook business pages, the de-facto business directory of Vietnam. Counts use exact category lists (coffee_shop, cafe, bubble_tea, tea_room, smoothie_juice_bar for "café-like"; the *_restaurant family plus diner, food_stand, food_truck, food_court for "restaurant-like") — never substring matching. Overture publishes a per-place confidence score; the page states how many venues clear 0.7. Facebook pages include closed businesses and duplicates, so treat the haze as "order of magnitude", not a census.

The "four lives" are a published crosswalk, not a vibe

Part 2 sorts every in-district Overture place into four kinds of street life by exact-prefix rules on the taxonomy hierarchy — never substring matches — with the full category→life table published as lives-crosswalk.json. The lives borrow their definitions: Sustenance = Gehl's necessary errands; Anchors = Jacobs's primary uses; Third places = Oldenburg (the cà phê is Vietnam's); Display = Gehl's optional, comparison-goods street. Pages too generic to read (bare "shopping", professional services, real estate, B2B) stay in an explicit unclassified bucket rather than being forced into a life. Same-name pages within 150 m are collapsed first (Meta service-area spam repeats one page dozens of times). Existence confidence in this snapshot is modest (median ≈ 0.55), so data.json bakes the four lives' counts at confidence ≥ 0.5 / 0.75 / 0.9 — and the table is honest about what moves: display is the biggest bucket of raw pages, but from ≥ 0.75 upward third places lead the street the data is surest actually exists. And one honest limit: all of this is fixed commerce. The itinerant sidewalk layer documented in Annette Kim's Sidewalk City (2015) — carts, baskets, evening stalls — is invisible to OSM and Overture alike. Sources: Gehl, Life Between Buildings (1971); Oldenburg, The Great Good Place (1989); Botta & Gutiérrez-Roig, PLoS One (2021); Gómez-Varo et al., Cities (2022); Gibert-Flutre, EPA (2018); Jamme, JAPA (2024).

Café "survival" is mapping churn, not business churn

We follow cafés across years by stable OSM node id. A café that was deleted and re-drawn as a new node counts as a death and a birth, so the survival numbers mix real closures with remapping. They say "the map churns like the street does" — they do not certify any particular closure.

“Chains vs độc lập” is a hub-first, walked-catchment model

Story 3 asks where retail chains land in Bình Thạnh. We take every Overture place whose brand matches a curated chain roster and whose existence confidence clears 0.75, then model reach two ways that matter. First, hub-first: before drawing any territory we cluster co-located outlets into shared-ground hubs on network (walked) distance, so that two chains 40 m apart on the same corner read as one shared knot rather than a knife-edge border that is really just geocode noise. Second, walked catchments: each outlet's 1 km reach follows the pedestrian street graph — the hẻm mesh included — not a circle, because nobody walks through walls. Every segment is then assigned to whichever seed reaches it first; segments two seeds reach within a small margin of each other are contested; segments no chain reaches within 1 km are unclaimed.

The model is pinned to these parameters (baked into chains.json, guarded by a test):

ParameterValueWhat it does
conf_min0.75minimum Overture existence confidence for an outlet to count
dedupe_m50 mcollapse same-brand duplicate pages within this radius (Meta service-area spam)
hub_cutoff_m200 mwalked distance under which outlets cluster into one shared hub
contested_margin_m30 mtwo reaches this close ⇒ the segment is a frontier, not owned
reach_m1000 mthe walked catchment radius — one kilometre on foot

Why 30 m, not 100? A sensitivity run re-pinned contested_margin_m from 100 to 30. The district is so chain-saturated that a 100 m margin painted 34% of all streets “contested”, which reads as mush; 30 m yields a legible 15.8% frontier while still honouring genuine ties.

The two reach models follow the network-buffer / network-Voronoi literature: walked (network) catchments rather than Euclidean circles after Oliver, Schuurman & Hall, Int. J. Health Geographics (2007); the first-reach tessellation is a generalized network Voronoi diagram after Okabe, Satoh & Sugihara, Int. J. Geographical Information Science (2008). A gravity (Huff) model was considered and rejected: it spreads probability mass everywhere and would have made the saturation point illegible, where a hard first-reach assignment makes the rare unclaimed streets pop.

Limitations. The walk graph treats street crossings as free, so a wide arterial like Điện Biên Phủ is crossed as if it were a footpath — real catchments are slightly smaller. Geocode jitter survives the dedupe; the curated roster mitigates brand-field noise but cannot erase a mis-placed pin. The independents are a partial view — Overture/Meta coverage, not a census of every shop — so the “beyond reach” count is a floor, not a tally. And the honest headline: Bình Thạnh is chain-saturated. Only 328 of 9,551 street segments (3.4%) and 121 of 25,158 independents (0.48%) lie beyond every chain's kilometre, so unclaimed is genuinely rare — that is the finding, not an artifact.

The Hub Atlas reads signals off the same data — none of them are definitions

The atlas profiles each of the 22 hubs the chains model already found. Every number on a hub page is inferred from the same Overture places and the same walked street graph as “Chains vs độc lập” — we never drew a new boundary or hand-labelled a hub. The catchment of a hub is unchanged: a place belongs to whichever hub owns the graph node it snaps to (the territory class from story 3). What the atlas adds is description, not new math.

Why it reads as a hub is two things, both inferred. Its street form comes from the walk graph: if the top two named streets near the centroid each carry ≥ 25% of the local edge length it is a crossroads; if one street dominates, a spine; if no street is named, a cluster. Its demand-generators — schools, clinics, offices, hotels, markets — are counted from the very same Overture places by exact taxonomy.primary sets (never substrings: “bar” must not catch “barber”). These say why a corner pulls a crowd; they are a reading of the places, not an independent survey.

Built-up is a footprint proxy, not population. The “built density” bar is the total building-footprint area (m²) inside a hub's catchment, min-max normalised across the 22 hubs so the busiest reads 1 and the emptiest 0. This is ground covered, not people: Overture's buildings here carry no usable floor count or height, so we cannot lift footprint into volume, and apartments are too sparsely tagged to split residential from commercial. Read the bar as “how much of this catchment is built over”, never as “how many live here”. The building footprints come from the Overture buildings release 2026-05-20.0, which may differ from the places release — built density is a coarse backdrop signal, and that small release gap is acceptable for it.

Hub relations are drawn from the same per-node reach distances. Two hubs share a frontier node when both reach it within the 1 km walked catchment and their distances to it fall within the 30 m contested margin — the identical tie rule the territory map uses. From the count of shared frontier nodes (and the hubs' four-lives mix) each adjacent pair is typed: compete when they share ≥ 20 frontier nodes (they fight for the same walkers); complement when they touch over fewer nodes but their four-lives mix is far apart (cosine distance ≥ 0.05 — adjacent, serving different needs); border otherwise. The two cut-offs (compete_min_nodes = 20, mix_far = 0.05) were swept over the 22 hubs and pinned to these values. One honest note on the cut-offs: Bình Thạnh is a homogeneous district, so adjacent hubs serve nearly the same four-lives mix and cosine distance never exceeds ≈ 0.14 — mix_far has to sit below that ceiling for complement to exist at all. With these values the bake splits the 86 adjacent pairs into 22 compete, 14 complement, and 50 border: most neighbours simply abut without strongly competing or diverging, which is the honest shape of a dense, similar district.

What can I eat here?

Each food place is sorted by the dish in its name — phở, bún, cơm, bánh mì, lẩu, ốc, cà phê — because Overture has no dish-level category (only vietnamese_restaurant, useless in Vietnam). A place that doesn't name its dish falls back to its Overture cuisine tag (Japanese, Korean, seafood…); only ~18% of food carries one, so the rest stay in Eateries. We map where the food that announces itself gathers — not a census of every kitchen. Each dot exposes all its raw Overture tags (primary, full hierarchy, alternates) so any classification is auditable.
